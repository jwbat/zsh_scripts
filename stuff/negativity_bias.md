The phrase *negativity bias* in spiritual practice borrows the term from psychology  
but gives it a distinctly contemplative meaning.

In psychology, negativity bias simply describes the well-documented tendency for negative experiences  
to have a stronger impact on the mind than positive ones of equal intensity —  
threats are registered more quickly and remembered more vividly than pleasures.

In spiritual contexts, the phrase is usually pointing to something more subtle and more  
consequential: the mind's habitual tendency to **contract around what is unpleasant**   
and to treat the absence of suffering as merely neutral rather than as a  
form of peace. This creates a kind of asymmetry in awareness — pain is  
vivid and attention-commanding, while wellbeing is taken for granted and passed over.

Teachers in the Theravāda and Tibetan traditions often describe this in terms of   
how the mind **appropriates experience**.  
Suffering gets elaborated — we spin stories, seek causes,  
resist the feeling — while moments of ease or clarity are barely noticed before attention  
moves on. The result is that the mind is, in a sense,  
trained by its own habits to dwell in suffering even when suffering is not strictly present.

In Advaita Vedanta and non-dual teachings, the framing is somewhat different.  
The negativity bias is seen as a consequence of **identification with the contracted self** — the ego.  
Because the ego is a structure of defense and boundary  
-maintenance, it is naturally vigilant toward threat. Peace, by contrast, is  
not something the ego *does* anything with; it can't manage  
or improve it. So the ego effectively filters it out. What you are  
prior to the ego — pure awareness — is itself undisturbed, but the conditioned  
mind never rests there long enough to recognize it.

In practical terms, teachers like Ramana Maharshi and Nisargadatta point to this when they  
observe that the natural state is already present but overlooked. The sadhana is less  
about acquiring something new and more about noticing what the negativity bias causes you to  
habitually skip past.

**What Vipassanā instructs with neutral experience**

The core instruction is deceptively simple: **notice it**. The problem with neutral  
vedanā is not that it's difficult to work with once seen — it  
's that it evades detection in the first place. So the primary practice is  
just to catch it, to recognize "this moment has a neutral tone"  
rather than letting awareness slide past it into the next more vivid stimulus.

More specifically, teachers in the Mahasi Sayadaw lineage would have you 
**note it** — mentally labeling "neutral" or simply observing the absence of pull or  
push — and then observe what the mind does next. Usually what follows is  
a subtle restlessness, a reaching for something more interesting. That reaching is itself the  
delusion anusaya activating. So neutral experience becomes a kind of trap door: if  
you can stay with it long enough, you catch the mind in the act of manufacturing dissatisfaction from nothing.

Shinzen Young frames it as training the *flavor* of neutrality to become interesting  
in its own right — equanimity not as flatness but as a kind of spacious  
okayness that, once you can actually taste it, turns out to be quietly satisfying.

**The deeper instruction**

The more advanced point is that neutral experience, when met with full attention rather  
than dullness, begins to reveal impermanence very clearly. Because there's no  
strong pleasant or unpleasant charge to fixate on, the bare arising and passing of  
phenomena becomes more visible. Neutral experience is, in a sense, the cleanest signal — least distorted by reactivity.

**What Zen brings**

Zen doesn't really have a parallel analytical structure here, but it addresses  
the same territory obliquely and in a way that's arguably more direct.  
Where Vipassanā says "notice neutral experience," Zen essentially says **there is no  
neutral experience** — or more precisely, the distinction between pleasant, unpleasant,  
and neutral is itself a conceptual overlay on something that is just *this*.

The koān tradition works partly by exhausting the mind's habitual tendency to categorize  
and react. When Zhaozhou is asked whether a dog has Buddha-nature and  
says *Mu* — that's not an answer that lands as pleasant  
, unpleasant, or neutral. It's a kind of deliberate short-circuit of the vedanā machinery altogether.

And Dōgen's instruction in *Fukanzazengi* to "think not-thinking"  
points at the same place from another angle. The mind sorts experience into  
categories worth attending to and categories not worth attending to. 
Zazen is, among other things, the practice of sitting in the middle of that sorting mechanism without  
operating it — which is perhaps the most radical possible response to the negativity bias, not correcting it but prior to it.

So Vipassanā works *with* the machinery carefully and systematically.  
Zen tends to  ignore the machinery entirely and point past it 
— which can feel frustrating until it suddenly doesn't.
